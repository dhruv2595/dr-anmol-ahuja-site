### Screening for Colon Disease

Multiple diseases can interfere with colon functioning, classified as benign or malignant, causing bleeding, infection, and perforation. Removing a colon segment generally doesn't affect normal function given the average person has 8-10 feet of small bowel and 3-5 feet of colon.

**Colorectal Cancer Screening Tests**

Screening identifies cancer in asymptomatic individuals. Tests divide into two categories:
- Stool-based tests examine feces for cancer signs—less invasive but require frequent repetition
- Visual exams assess colon and rectum structure using scopes or imaging

**Stool-Based Tests**

*Fecal Immunochemical Test (FIT)* searches for occult blood in stool, reacting to hemoglobin in red blood cells. Small stool amounts collected on cards/tubes at home. No dietary restrictions apply unlike guaiac-based tests. Performed annually. Positive results require colonoscopy investigation.

*Guaiac-Based Fecal Occult Blood Test (gFOBT)* detects hidden blood through chemical reaction. Cannot distinguish colon bleeding from other digestive tract sources. Annual testing required. Home kit instructions must be followed carefully.

Pre-test restrictions include: avoiding NSAIDs for 7 days (prevent false-positive bleeding), limiting vitamin C to under 250mg for 3 days, and avoiding red meats for 3 days.

*Stool DNA Test* identifies abnormal DNA sections from cancer or polyp cells. Cologuard®, the only test currently available, tests for both DNA changes and blood in the stool. Performed every 3 years.

**Visual Exams**

*Colonoscopy* allows complete colon and rectum examination via colonoscope — a flexible tube about the width of a finger with a light and small video camera on the end. Procedure lasts approximately 30 minutes but may extend if polyps require removal. Sedation is administered intravenously. Bowel preparation beforehand is mandatory. Transportation home is required post-procedure due to sedation effects.

Pre-test: Disclose all medications. Complete bowel cleansing required via laxative solutions, pills, or enemas.

During procedure: Patient lies on side with knees up. Doctor inserts lubricated colonoscope through anus into rectum, advancing to the cecum. Air inflation aids visualization. Polyps are removed and biopsied when necessary.

Side effects include bloating, gas pains, cramping post-procedure, and rare serious complications including perforation requiring surgical repair.

*CT Colonography (Virtual Colonoscopy)* combines multiple CT scan images creating 2D and 3D views for polyp/cancer detection. Useful for individuals unable to tolerate invasive procedures. Requires identical bowel preparation as colonoscopy. A small flexible tube fills the colon with air. If abnormalities appear, colonoscopy becomes necessary.

During test: Takes approximately 10 minutes. Patient drinks contrast solution potentially, lies on table, receives air inflation via rectal tube, and undergoes imaging while holding breath for 15 seconds (typically twice — supine and prone positions).

Side effects remain minimal compared to colonoscopy. Bloating and cramping may occur. Radiation exposure is minimal. Perforation risk is substantially lower than colonoscopy.

*Flexible Sigmoidoscopy* examines the rectum and lower colon portion using a sigmoidoscope (a flexible, lighted tube about the thickness of a finger with a small video camera on the end). Not widely used for colorectal cancer screening in the US.

Before test: Disclose medications and complete bowel cleansing via diet, enemas, or laxatives.

During procedure: Typically 10-20 minutes, sedation optional. Patient lies on left side with knees near chest. Doctor may examine rectum with gloved finger beforehand. Sigmoidoscope insertion follows lubrication. Air inflation aids visualization. Discomfort includes pressure and cramping sensible through deep mouth-breathing.

Complications remain uncommon but include minor bleeding and rare perforation.
